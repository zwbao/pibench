# PIBench: Can Agents Run a Research Lab?

*A long-horizon agent benchmark in the spirit of CEO-Bench.*

**Abstract.** Language-model agents are increasingly competent at short, well-specified
tasks, but the consequential work of a career is not a task — it is a long chain of
interdependent decisions made under uncertainty, where feedback is slow and the ground
keeps shifting. We introduce **PIBench**, which stresses these capabilities together by
simulating one of the most information-poor, delayed-reward jobs there is: running an
academic research lab. An agent plays a new assistant professor who, over **60 simulated
months**, must recruit students whose true ability is hidden behind noisy and
pool-biased signals, place multi-year bets on research topics whose popularity drifts and
occasionally booms or busts, win grants from agencies with hidden preferences, and steer
projects to publication — all while managing two scarce resources at once: **money and a
fixed monthly budget of the PI's own attention**. Success is measured by research
**Impact**: citations accrued plus a going-concern projection of published papers' future
citations, a score designed so that abandoning the lab to hoard cash earns nothing.
Every outcome is generated by explicit mechanistic rules, never an LLM judge, and the
world is partially observable, non-stationary, and full of delayed, coupled consequences.
Across seven models served through Alibaba's Bailian platform, we find that most current
models struggle to sustain a productive lab: only the strongest models clear a simple non-LLM baseline, none approach an oracle upper bound, and scores are dominated by a heavy-tailed variance that a few seeds cannot resolve. PIBench takes a step toward
measuring whether agents can compound small, uncertain investments into lasting progress.

## 1. Introduction

Agents built on today's language models can fix a GitHub issue, follow a support policy,
or complete a web workflow. These are real skills, and they share a shape: a clear goal,
a short horizon, and quick feedback. As agents saturate this shape, the interesting
question is what they do when the local task is no longer the bottleneck — when success
depends on *sustaining* progress toward a distant goal as earlier decisions keep shaping
later ones.

Human intellectual achievement is rarely a single well-specified task. Running a research
lab is a canonical example of the opposite: a five-to-six-year arc in which almost every
important variable is hidden, almost every payoff is delayed, and the environment does not
hold still. A new professor cannot observe how good a PhD applicant really is, only noisy
transcripts and inflated letters. She cannot observe which research topics will be hot in
two years, only this month's headlines and preprint counts. She cannot observe what a
funding agency wants, only whether last year's proposal was funded. Her decisions —
whom to hire, what to work on, when to publish, which grants to chase — commit resources
that cannot be recovered, and their consequences (citations, reputation, a student's
morale collapsing into a departure) surface months or years later. And the whole system
is coupled: a quality failure in one project bleeds into the lab's reputation, which
changes who applies next year.

This is exactly the combination that recent long-horizon benchmarks have begun to probe.
**CEO-Bench** (Chen et al., 2026) pioneered this direction by having an agent run a
startup for 500 days through a programmable interface over a mechanistic market. PIBench
adopts CEO-Bench's core recipe — long horizon, hidden state observed only through noisy
indirect signals, delayed and coupled consequences, a non-stationary world, and fully
mechanistic rules so that success depends on genuine skill rather than talking an LLM
judge into a good outcome — and points it at a different, in some ways harsher, corner of
the world. Three design choices distinguish it:

1. **A second scarce resource: the PI's attention.** Beyond money, the agent has exactly
   100 hours a month, use-it-or-lose-it, to split across mentoring, writing proposals,
   polishing papers, interviewing candidates, and service. A lab can be cash-rich and
   time-poor. This dual-resource pressure is absent from single-currency simulators.
2. **People as the central, illiquid asset.** The lab's output comes from students whose
   ability must be *inferred* from biased signals at hiring time, whose skill grows with
   mentoring, and whose morale follows a dynamics that can cascade into quitting at the
   worst moment. Stipends are multi-year commitments; you cannot fire a student to cut
   costs. Investments in people cannot be liquidated.
3. **A harvest-resistant score.** CEO-Bench grades on final cash, which we found
   incentivizes the strongest agents to stop operating and coast to a liquidation. PIBench
   grades on Impact = citations to date plus a deterministic projection of published
   papers' future citations, so a paper published in the final months still "earns," and
   the winning move is to keep the research engine running, not to shut it down.

We also adopt three methodological lessons from a close reading of CEO-Bench's
weaknesses: we report the **mean across multiple seeds** rather than a best-of-N run; we
tune the non-LLM baseline on **held-out** seeds rather than the evaluation seeds; and we
report **token cost** alongside every score so that "spent more thinking" is not confused
with "is more capable."

Our evaluation on seven Bailian-served models shows the task is hard for current agents:
Impact spans a 15-fold range across models; the two weakest fail to beat a non-LLM heuristic; and per-seed variance is so large — one model scored 2106, 160, and 158 on its three worlds — that catching a single research "boom" dominates a five-year career. We release the simulator, the harness, an adversarial test suite, and all
trajectories.


## 2. Designing PIBench

### 2.1 How PIBench works

An agent runs a fictional lab for **60 months**. It begins month 1 with a **$600,000
startup fund, zero students, and baseline reputation**, and is graded on Impact at month
60. If the lab's budget ever falls below zero, the lab **collapses** and the run ends,
keeping only the citations earned so far.

**Two budgets.** Cash flows in from grants and out through stipends, compute, travel, and
overhead. Separately, the PI has **100 hours of attention per month**; every mentoring
allocation, interview, proposal, and paper revision draws it down, and whatever is unused
expires. A mandatory service duty removes 12 hours once a year. The core tension of the
job is that the two budgets bind at different times: early on you have cash but no results
to build on; later you have results but not enough hours to mentor five students, write
two proposals, and polish three papers in the same month.

**What an agent does.** Each month the agent takes unlimited (harness-capped) actions
across roughly 26 tools in eight namespaces — recruiting and interviewing, assigning
students and compute to projects, choosing project ambition (tier 1/2/3), polishing and
submitting drafts to four venues, writing grant proposals, doing market/field research,
responding to events, and reading the lab's database with SQL — then advances the clock.

**How money and Impact are made.** Students on projects generate drafts whose latent
quality depends on team skill, project novelty, compute, and polish. Drafts submitted to
venues are accepted or rejected by a noisy review, and accepted papers accrue citations
over years following a ramp-and-decay curve modulated by topic hotness and the lab's
reputation. Citations are the reward, and because they lag publication by a year or more,
the headline score includes a deterministic going-concern projection so that late,
high-quality papers are properly valued.

### 2.2 What makes it rigorous and hard

**Granular, mechanistic simulation.** Twenty-six customer-like *research topics* and
individual students are simulated as objects with their own hidden parameters. Whether a
paper is accepted, whether a student quits, whether a grant is funded — all are decided
by explicit formulas and stochastic draws (Normal, Poisson, Bernoulli, Log-normal), never
by an LLM asked to role-play a reviewer. This closes the failure mode where an agent talks
a simulated judge into an unearned reward.

**Hidden information and indirect feedback.** The agent sees only what a real PI could:
dashboards, database records, a public news/preprint feed, conference reports, reviewer
scores, and monthly student reports. It never sees true ability, latent paper quality,
topic hotness, agency preferences, or student morale. It must *infer* these — that a given
applicant pool inflates its letters, that a topic is heating up, that an agency favors a
theme — from noisy traces.

**Delayed and coupled consequences.** Research takes months; citations take years; grant
decisions lag proposals by up to half a year; a scoop is revealed only after it has
already damaged a project. Reputation propagates: a graduating student lifts next year's
applicant pool and adds citation spillover to the whole lab, while a morale collapse in
one student pulls down the group. No single lever can be isolated and hill-climbed.

**Non-stationarity.** Topic hotness follows a drifting mean-reverting process punctuated
by booms and busts; crowding follows hotness with a lag and drives scooping; a
macroeconomic funding climate cycles over years; competitors implicitly raise the bar.
A strategy that worked in year 1 need not work in year 4.

### 2.3 A programmable interface

Following CEO-Bench, the agent operates through a Python package (`pilab`) executed in a
terminal, not a fixed menu of tool calls. It writes code that composes the API with SQL
queries and its own analysis — for example, joining the submissions and student tables to
decide who should revise which paper, or estimating topic momentum from the preprint feed.
A 19-table observable database backs the world. This makes *information acquisition itself*
a tested capability: the strongest agents mine the database and the news feed to
reconstruct hidden structure, rather than acting on the dashboard alone.

**Harness.** Context is refreshed every simulated month; only the agent's self-maintained
memory file persists (≤ 6,000 characters). Long-horizon coherence must therefore live in
what the agent chooses to write down. Each month it gets up to eight code-execution turns
against a hardened sandbox, then the clock auto-advances.


## 3. Experiments

### 3.1 Setup

We evaluate seven models served through Alibaba's Bailian (DashScope) platform,
spanning a range of capability tiers: qwen3.7-max, qwen3.7-plus, qwen-plus, deepseek-v3.2, glm-5.2, kimi-k2.6, and qwen-turbo. Each model runs the **same three
world seeds** (101, 102, 103); the headline number is the **mean Impact across seeds**,
and we report the per-seed numbers, never a best-of-N. A non-LLM **rule-based baseline**
is tuned by a small grid search on separate seeds (1001, 1002) and evaluated on the held-out
seeds, so it never sees the test worlds during tuning. We also report an **oracle** upper
bound: a policy that plays with full access to hidden state (true abilities, the future
hotness trajectory, latent quality), used only to bound attainable headroom.

### 3.2 Results

**Table 1. Benchmark summary.** Impact is mean across seeds {101, 102, 103}; per-seed
values show the spread. Reference rows use the same three seeds. Because three seeds are
high-variance, we also report broader eight-seed estimates: baseline **235**, oracle
**1624**.

| Model | Impact (mean) | per-seed | collapse | pubs | grants | tokens |
|---|--:|--:|--:|--:|--:|--:|
| qwen3.7-max | **808** | 2106 / 160 / 158 | 0/3 | 10.7 | 2.7 | 0.94M |
| glm-5.2 | **695** | 1818 / 251 / 16 | 1/3 | 8.0 | 3.7 | 2.06M |
| kimi-k2.6 | **556** | 1151 / 486 / 30 | 1/3 | 7.3 | 2.0 | 2.27M |
| qwen3.7-plus | **394** | 1033 / 125 / 24 | 1/3 | 5.3 | 2.0 | 1.30M |
| qwen-plus | **222** | 246 / 237 / 182 | 0/3 | 5.3 | 1.0 | 0.54M |
| deepseek-v3.2 | **58** | 159 / 14 / 0 | 0/3 | 2.0 | 2.0 | 2.16M |
| qwen-turbo | **54** | 139 / 0 / 23 | 0/3 | 1.3 | 0.7 | 1.47M |
| *rule-based baseline* | 59 | — | 1/3 | 7.0 | 1.3 | no LLM |
| *oracle (full state)* | 878 | 2279 / 315 / 38 | 1/3 | 9.3 | 5.0 | upper bnd |

Impact spans a 15-fold range, from qwen3.7-max (808) to qwen-turbo (54), so PIBench
discriminates cleanly across capability tiers. The two weakest models fail to beat even
the simple non-LLM baseline on the same worlds; the strongest four clear it by a wide
margin. No model approaches the oracle's broader ceiling (1624).

**The scores are dominated by variance, and the variance is structure, not noise.**
qwen3.7-max's three runs scored 2106 / 160 / 158 — a single seed drives its average — and
the oracle shows the same signature (2279 / 315 / 38). Most of a lab's Impact comes from
catching a topic *boom*, a rare high-magnitude event, so a career's payoff is heavy-tailed,
much as real academic impact is. This is exactly why we report per-seed spread and a mean
rather than a best-of-N: with three seeds the ranking of adjacent models is not settled,
and it is a caution for any long-horizon benchmark that reports a single number. One model,
qwen-plus, is the informative exception — 246 / 237 / 182, low variance and no collapse:
it never catches a boom but never crashes, a conservative strategy reminiscent of
CEO-Bench's cash-preserving models.

### 3.3 A look into agent behavior

Behavioral traces separate the models by more than the headline number. **Action
intensity** (actions per simulated month) varies about three-fold but does *not* track
Impact: deepseek-v3.2 takes 3.1 actions/month — as many as qwen3.7-max — yet scores 58 to
its 808. **Conversion** (papers per student-year) tracks the leaderboard far better:
qwen3.7-max extracts 0.89 papers per student-year against deepseek's 0.39. The gap is not
in taking actions — every model issues valid tool calls — but in composing mentoring,
project choice, polishing, and venue targeting into a paper that gets in and gets cited.
This echoes CEO-Bench's observation that weak agents produce valid individual actions yet
fail to coordinate them into outcomes over a long horizon.


## 4. Ablations

Running the deterministic rule-based baseline under modified world configurations isolates
where the difficulty comes from (eight seeds each):

| World variant | Impact mean | median | collapse |
|---|--:|--:|--:|
| default (60mo) | 237 | 156 | 2/8 |
| no non-stationarity | 104 | 102 | 2/8 |
| no scooping | 266 | 153 | 2/8 |
| short horizon (12mo) | 2 | 0 | 0/8 |
| mid horizon (30mo) | 17 | 10 | 0/8 |

The clearest signal is the **horizon**: shrinking the run from 60 to 30 to 12 months
collapses Impact from 237 to 17 to 2. This is the delayed-reward structure made visible —
citations accrue over years, so a short-horizon lab publishes too late to earn almost
anything, however well it is run. Turning off **non-stationarity** (freezing hotness, no
booms/busts) roughly halves Impact (237 → 104): in PIBench the drifting field is as much
opportunity as hazard, and much of the upside is committing to a topic before it booms. An
**ambitious** policy that hires to capacity and runs only tier-3 projects bankrupts the lab
on most seeds — overreach without runway discipline is punished, the research analog of
CEO-Bench's hire-everyone collapse.


## 5. Related work

PIBench sits alongside a line of long-horizon and economically-grounded agent evaluations.
**CEO-Bench** (Chen et al., 2026) is the closest relative and direct inspiration: a
500-day mechanistic startup simulation with hidden, noisy, delayed dynamics. Where CEO-Bench
tests operating a business, PIBench tests running a research organization, and adds a second
scarce resource (attention), an illiquid people-centric asset base, and a harvest-resistant
score. **Vending-Bench** (Backlund and Petersson, 2025) and **AccountingBench** (Penrose,
2025) established long-horizon business tasks but involve narrower decisions or more
stable, observable environments. Earlier agent benchmarks — SWE-bench, WebArena, τ-bench,
GAIA, GDPval — evaluate valuable real-world skills but at short horizons with quickly
observed outcomes or one-shot deliverables. Memory and continual-learning benchmarks test
retention over time but usually reduce to information retrieval rather than a persistent
enterprise steered under uncertainty.


## 6. Limitations and conclusion

**Limitations.** We approximate a research career, and gaps remain between the model and
reality. Product quality of research is abstracted to a scalar; we do not evaluate the
substance of ideas, only their modeled impact. We omit teaching load, collaboration
politics, ethics/compliance, and the human texture of mentorship beyond a morale scalar.
Each model is run on three seeds — enough to report a mean with per-seed spread, but the
variance is real and the ranking of close models is not statistically settled; more seeds
would sharpen it. Finally, results are entangled with the harness and the model's
willingness to spend tokens; we report cost to keep this visible, but a model that acts
more per month has an advantage that is partly effort, not only capability.

**Conclusion.** PIBench shows a gap between models' local tool competence and the
sustained, adaptive judgment a five-year project demands: agents can take plausible
individual actions — interview a candidate, submit a paper, write a proposal — but
struggle to compound them into a lab that grows under delayed feedback, hidden state, and a
shifting field. Measuring that gap is the point. To build agents that do more than answer
requests — that can steer a long-running effort through uncertainty — we need environments
that ask for exactly that, and PIBench is one step toward them.

## Appendix A. Simulator mechanics (summary)

See `SPEC.md` in the repository for the complete, normative specification. In brief:

- **Topics & field.** 8 topics; hotness `H_k` is a log-space Ornstein–Uhlenbeck process
  with boom/bust jumps; crowding `C_k` lags hotness and drives scooping and novelty decay.
  Observed only via a Poisson news feed, a preprint feed, and lagged conference reports.
- **Students.** 4 applicant pools with different hidden ability distributions and signal
  biases (e.g. international letters inflated, nontraditional transcripts noisy with rare
  gems). Skill grows with mentoring; morale is a mean-reverting state driven by paper
  outcomes, scoops, overload, and support; quit hazard rises as morale falls. Stipends are
  committed; PhDs graduate after ≥48 months and ≥2 papers, and alumni lift future
  applicants and add citation spillover.
- **Projects & papers.** Tier 1/2/3 require increasing work and yield better papers; tier 3
  risks dead ends. Draft quality uses the *time-averaged* team and compute over the
  project's life (so late roster or compute manipulation buys nothing). Four venues
  (top/mid conference, workshop, journal) with different acceptance bars, decision delays,
  publication cadence, and visibility. Citations follow a ramp-then-decay curve scaled by
  hotness and reputation.
- **Grants.** Four agencies (BSF, AMP, TIF, FEL) with hidden topic preferences, distinct
  schedules, award sizes, decision delays, acceptance bars, and concurrency caps; a
  macroeconomic funding climate cycles over years and is observed only through delayed,
  noisy news.
- **Determinism.** Independent per-subsystem RNG streams plus entity-keyed draws guarantee
  that the same seed and policy reproduce exactly, and that unrelated actions cannot shift
  another subsystem's outcomes.

## Appendix B. Adversarial validation

Before running experiments we subjected the simulator and harness to a multi-agent
adversarial review across seven lenses (spec conformance, state-machine correctness,
hidden-information leaks, economic exploits, scoring exploits, determinism, harness
security), with every finding independently verified by a skeptic. This surfaced and we
fixed, among others: a sandbox escape that exposed the entire hidden world state
(including the future hotness trajectory) and allowed direct score mutation; an uncapped
reputation-farming exploit that scored several times the baseline with zero students; a
one-month compute-spike exploit on draft quality; and an order-dependence in review
outcomes that violated determinism. The hardened sandbox and the resulting invariant and
penetration test suites ship with the benchmark.
